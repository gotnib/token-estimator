Why Your AI Prompts Are 40% Longer Than They Need to Be

May 2026 · 7 min read · By TokenLens

After analyzing thousands of AI prompts, a pattern emerges consistently: the average prompt is about 40% longer than it needs to be. Not because users are padding deliberately — but because natural language carries habits that don't translate well to AI instructions.

Here are the 8 categories of token waste we see most often, with real before/after examples for each.

1. Politeness overhead (avg. 18% of waste)

Phrases: please, could you, I was wondering, thank you, I appreciate it, feel free to
Avg. 18% of total tokens in polite prompts
AI models don't respond better to politeness. They don't have feelings to spare. Every "please" and "thank you" is a token that returns nothing.
❌ 28 tokens
Could you please help me write a short professional bio? I'd really appreciate it if you could keep it under 100 words.
✓ 14 tokens
Write a professional bio under 100 words.

2. Hedging language (avg. 14% of waste)

Phrases: I'm not sure but, maybe you could, if it's possible, if you think it's appropriate
Avg. 14% of total tokens in hedged prompts
Hedging introduces ambiguity without adding information. If you have a requirement, state it directly. If it's truly optional, say "optionally" or leave it out.
❌ 32 tokens
If possible, could you maybe add some examples? I'm not sure if that would be helpful but it might make it clearer.
✓ 5 tokens
Include 2-3 examples.

3. Redundancy — saying it twice (avg. 22% of waste)

Repeating the same requirement in different words
Avg. 22% of total tokens in verbose prompts — the largest single category
This is the biggest category of waste. "Simple, clear, and easy to understand" is three ways of saying the same thing. "Concise and brief" is redundant. Pick one.
❌ 22 tokens
Keep the explanation simple, clear, easy to understand, and accessible to someone without a technical background.
✓ 10 tokens
Explain simply for non-technical readers.

4. Unnecessary context (avg. 19% of waste)

Background the model doesn't need to complete the task
Avg. 19% of total tokens when context is over-explained
You don't need to explain your entire situation. Give the model what it needs to do the job — not your full backstory.
❌ 52 tokens
I'm currently working on a project for my company. We've been trying to improve our customer service process and I've been tasked with writing some email templates. I need help writing an email to apologize to a customer for a delayed shipment.
✓ 14 tokens
Write a customer email apologizing for a delayed shipment.

5. Filler opening phrases (avg. 12% of waste)

Phrases: what I'm trying to do is, the thing is, basically, so essentially
Avg. 12% of tokens in conversational prompts
These phrases are verbal habits from human conversation that serve no purpose in text instructions. Just start with the instruction.
❌ 24 tokens
So basically what I need is for you to summarize this article. What I'm trying to do is get the main points.
✓ 7 tokens
Summarize this article's main points.

6. Restating the obvious (avg. 8% of waste)

Telling the model things it already knows: "As an AI language model, you should...", "Using your knowledge of...", "Given that you have access to information about...". The model knows what it is. Skip it.

7. Over-specified output format (avg. 6% of waste)

When you specify format in multiple ways: "Please provide the answer as a numbered list. Each item should be on its own line. Use numbers like 1, 2, 3." — "Numbered list" already covers all of this.

8. Closing pleasantries (avg. 1% of waste)

Small but still waste: "Thanks in advance!", "Looking forward to your response!", "Any help would be greatly appreciated!" These cost tokens and return nothing.

The compound effect at scale

These categories seem small individually, but they compound. A 200-token system prompt with all 8 patterns typically optimizes to ~115 tokens — a 42.5% reduction. At 50,000 API calls per month, that's 4.25 million fewer input tokens. At Claude Sonnet pricing, that's $12.75/month saved from one prompt change.

Multiply that across a production application with 5-10 different prompts and the monthly savings become meaningful.

The principle behind it all

A well-written AI prompt gives the model:

  1. What to do (the task)
  2. What to use (the relevant context)
  3. What format to return (if non-obvious)
  4. What constraints apply (if any)

Everything else is waste. Not because brevity is a virtue in itself — a specific 60-word prompt is better than a vague 15-word one. But specificity and length are different things. You can be extremely specific in very few tokens.
