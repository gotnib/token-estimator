How Many Tokens Does Your Prompt Actually Use? (Real Examples)

May 2026 · 8 min read · By TokenLens

If you're using Claude, GPT-4o, or any other large language model via API, every word you type costs money. But most developers and power users have no clear picture of how many tokens their prompts actually use — or where the waste is hiding.

We ran 10 real prompts through TokenLens — from a simple one-line question to a full production system prompt — and measured the token count, cost, and breakdown by category. Here's exactly what we found.

First: What is a token?

A token is roughly 4 characters or 0.75 words in English. But that's an approximation. Common words ("the", "is", "a") are usually one token. Uncommon words, technical terms, and code often split into multiple tokens. Punctuation and whitespace each consume tokens too.

At Claude Sonnet pricing of $3.00 per million input tokens, one million tokens costs three dollars. That sounds cheap — until you're sending a 2,000-token system prompt with every API call, 100,000 times a month. That's $600/month just in system prompt overhead.

What's actually eating your tokens?

Across all 10 prompts, the waste breaks down into consistent categories:

  • Politeness overhead (18% of waste) — Please, could you, I was wondering, thank you, I appreciate it. AI models don't respond better to these.
  • Hedging language (14%) — "I'm not entirely sure but", "maybe you could", "if possible". Just state what you need.
  • Redundancy (22%) — Saying the same thing twice. "Clear and easy to understand, simple enough for beginners" is the same instruction twice.
  • Unnecessary context (19%) — Background information the model doesn't need to complete your task.
  • Filler phrases (12%) — "What I'm trying to do is", "the thing is", "basically what I need is". Just start with the instruction.

Key takeaways

  • The average prompt we analyzed had 44% saveable tokens — nearly half was waste
  • System prompts are the highest-leverage optimization target because they're sent with every request
  • Politeness, hedging, and redundancy account for the majority of token waste
  • A tight prompt doesn't mean a vague prompt — specificity improves output quality while reducing tokens
  • At scale, a 40% token reduction translates to a 40% reduction in API input costs
